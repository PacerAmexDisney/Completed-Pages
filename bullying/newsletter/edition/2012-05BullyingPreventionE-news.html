BULLYING PREVENTION E-NEWS – May 2012

Save the Date:  Wednesday, Oct. 10 is Unity Day!

Mark your calendars now for Unity Day on Wednesday, Oct. 10, 2012, during PACER’s Sixth Annual National Bullying Prevention Month. On Unity Day, people across America – including celebrities like Ellen DeGeneres – wear orange and link together in schools, communities, and online to send a meaningful message of support for those who have been bullied. If you want to help end bullying in your community, make plans to take part in Unity Day. ‘Attend’ and ‘Share’ the Unity Day Facebook Event with family and friends so everyone can ‘Make it Orange and Make it End!’ this October. PACER Provides Colorful Ways to Get Involved

Bullying is no fun but learning about it can be. One of the most popular resources available for elementary school children (pre-K to 3rd grade) is the “Color the Crew” coloring book, which gives teachers and students a wonderful opportunity to talk about bullying while they have fun. Each page includes an important message about respecting others, valuing differences, and being a friend. This is just one of many tools from PACER’s National Bullying Prevention Center that you can use to become involved in bullying prevention. 

Learn the Facts About Bullying of Students with Disabilities

It’s a fact: Children with disabilities are much more likely to be bullied than their non-disabled peers. One study indicates that 60 percent of students with disabilities report being bullied regularly compared with 25 percent of all students. Most research reveals that children with disabilities are two to three times more likely to be bullied. Parents, students, and educators can learn more by reading the “Top 10 Facts on Bullying and Harassment of Students with Disabilities,” which includes links to a multitude of valuable resources.

President Obama Endorses Legislation on Bullying

President Barack Obama has officially endorsed the proposed federal Student Non-Discrimination Act (SNDA) and the Safe Schools Improvement Act (SSIA), which would target discrimination and bullying against students based on their sexual orientation and gender identity. The “Bully” movie was shown at the White House in April to key Obama Administration staff, including Secretary of Education Arne Duncan. More

Teens Tune In to the Power of Music

Music is a powerful tool and teens are using it to amplify the bullying prevention message. The music video for Keenan West’s song “Never Ever” includes 40 youth from an Ohio middle school, who drew on their own experiences with bullying to make the video more realistic. “It was so rewarding to see how they related and responded to the song’s message,” says West, who is donating 50 cents from every digital download of the song to PACER’s National Bullying Prevention Center. “I was one of the kids in school standing on the sidelines and what I realize now is that I was just as wrong as the bullies themselves. We need to make it cool to stand up to bullying.” More

Alternative Rocker Comes Through for the Cause

Supporters everywhere are coming up with unique and creative ways to raise money for bullying prevention. Indie Music Channel Award winner Melia Maccarone and her alternative rock band played a concert in Rochester, N.Y., and donated all of the proceeds to PACER’s National Bullying Prevention Center. Melia was bullied in both middle school and high school, but music has helped her discover the positives of being different. She staged the show as a way to help other kids who have experienced bullying. Are you doing something original to contribute to the cause?
