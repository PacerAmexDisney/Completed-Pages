Heads Up!

In September 2011, Minnesota's Youth Sports Concussion Law went into effect. The goal of this legislation is to improve the recognition of – and response to – concussion injuries within all statewide youth athletic activities. By ensuring that athletes do not go back into games or practice too soon after sustaining a concussion, preventable and potentially more serious brain injuries can be avoided. Some studies show that the effects of a concussion can last up to a year, and children and youth are more vulnerable than adults.  The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention provides a free online course for coaches, parents, and others who are helping to keep athletes safe from concussion.

Statewide Health Improvement Program Progress Report

Minnesota's nationally-recognized Statewide Health Improvement Program (SHIP) released a progress report in March which detailed partnerships and activities in community-wide public health and prevention. SHIP is a program designed to improve health and reduce health care costs. One strategy is to work in partnership with schools. For example, “Farm to School” efforts are increasing access to local produce in at least 367 schools and 22 school districts in Minnesota. These efforts are serving healthy, local food to more than 200,000 students across Minnesota. Also, more than 110 schools, with 77,000 students state-wide, are actively engaged in Safe Routes to School, a program that supports students walking or biking to and from school.

Bullying and Food Allergy

Research suggests that about one-third of children with food allergies are teased or harassed at school because of their health condition. Children who are different from others in their school or neighborhood are often singled out for verbal ridicule, social isolation, or even physical abuse. In the case of food allergies, the bullying sometimes includes threats involving allergy-producing food items.  PACER’s National Bullying Prevention Center offers numerous resources to help prevent bullying.

The National Center for Ease of Use of Community-Based Services works to advance policy and practice solutions that improve the ease of use of community-based services for families with a child with special health care needs (CSHCN). Medical Home Philosophy of Care
Author: PACER Center

Many clinics in Minnesota are becoming certified health care homes, or “medical homes.” A clinic that uses the medical home philosophy of care can be a great resource for families of children with special health care needs and disabilities. A medical home is not a building, house, or hospital, but a better, family-centered way to provide care for children with special health care needs and disabilities. In a medical home, a staff member works to coordinate care between your pediatrician or family practice physician and all of the medical and non-medical services your child needs. Even if your doctor is not a certified medical home, you can still benefit from some of these ideas.
