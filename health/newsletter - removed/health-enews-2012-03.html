Can Your Child Have PCA Services in Minnesota?

It's a common question: How do you access Personal Care Assistance (PCA) services for your child with a disability or special health care need? To gain PCA services, applicants must already receive Medical Assistance (MA) or be eligible to receive MA services, and must undergo an assessment process. During a PCA assessment, the professional will look for proof that the child or youth needs hands-on physical assistance, cuing, or constant supervision to complete activities of daily living (ADLs). They will also look for behaviors, such as aggression, that require constant supervision. Parents don't need to be discouraged by the process!

Sick at school

Some studies estimate that a third or more of U.S. schools have mold, dust, and other indoor air problems serious enough to aggravate conditions like allergy and asthma in students and teachers. Chemicals used in schools, such as pesticides and cleaning products, may contribute to these respiratory issues as well. You can work with the school to make sure your child's health care needs are not aggravated by the school environment. Begin by discussing the issue with the school nurse using PACER's sample health plans. How Schools Can Involve Parents in Health Activities

Parent Engagement: Strategies for Involving Parents in School Health is a new resource from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's Division of Adolescent and School Health. It brings together research and best practices related to parent engagement in schools and describes strategies for connecting with parents, engaging parents in school health activities, and sustaining parent engagement.
