School lunches look different this fall

Because of changes to federal nutrition standards this year, school lunches this fall will be healthier. Some of the changes include offering both fruits and vegetables every day of the week and only fat-free or low-fat milk varieties, increased whole grain-rich foods, different portion sizes, and food with reduced saturated fat, trans fats, and sodium. More nutritious meals at school are an important way that schools can help children be healthy. Ideas about healthy living

Flu shots and children with disabilities

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) is urging parents of children with disabilities and special health care needs to have their child receive a flu vaccine early this year. A recently published study showed that children with neurologic conditions are at greater risk for complications from the flu. Find information about receiving a flu shot in Minnesota or contact your child’s doctor.

Sleepy teenager?

Researchers say that teens need at least nine hours of sleep each night, and that school-aged children should have 10 to 12 hours. As part of the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Healthy People 2020 efforts, new resources about sleep have been added to healthfinder.gov. It can be a challenge to make sure that children and teens have enough sleep, especially when taking medications. If you suspect that your child or teen has a sleep problem, work with your family physician, and share information with your child’s school. More about sleep and school performance

United Healthcare family grants

Families needing financial assistance to help pay for children’s health care treatments, services, and equipment can receive up to $5,000 from the United Healthcare Children’s Foundation. Grants are provided to help fill the gap between what is covered by commercial health insurance and what the family can pay in out-of-pocket expenses. Families are required to demonstrate financial eligibility. Apply for family grants

Scholarship supports study of health and disabilities


The American Association on Health and Disability (AAHD) is accepting applications for the AAHD Scholarship Program (2012-2013), which supports students with disabilities pursuing higher education in a field related to disability and health. Preference is given to students who plan to pursue undergraduate/graduate studies in the field of public health, behavioral health, epidemiology, health promotion, disability studies, to include disability policy and disability research. Scholarships are limited to under $1,000 and are competitive each year. Visit the AAHD website to download application. Deadline for submission is November 15, 2012. For more information, contact Maria Manolatos, AAHD Scholarship Coordinator, at mmanolatos@aahd.us.

Comcast offers low-cost Internet access and computers for families

Minnesota families with children who receive free or reduced-price school lunches through the National School Lunch Program may be eligible for Internet Essentials through Comcast. Eligible families receive residential Internet service for $9.95 per month plus tax, with no price increases, no activation fees, and no equipment rental fees. They also have access to free training on how to use the Internet – online, in print, and in person. Families may also purchase a low-cost computer when they sign up for Internet Essentials for $149.99 plus tax. The toll-free number is 1-855-8-INTERNET (1-855-846-8376). Spanish speakers can call 1-855-SOLO-995 (1-855-765-6995). Find out if your family is eligible for free or reduced-price school lunches
