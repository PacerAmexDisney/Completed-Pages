Your help is needed today: Bill removing most MN Special Education laws is introduced!
Senator John Pederson (SD 15—St Cloud and surrounding communities) has introduced SF 1291. This bill repeals, or eliminates most of the sections of Minnesota law that tell schools and districts how to serve students with disabilities.  In addition to the 54 sections of Minnesota law eliminated, the bill also removes 28 parts of special education rules that give further guidance to schools and districts. It would repeal most Minnesota special education law and rule, which provide needed protections and exceed Federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) law. This bill, if enacted, would cause delays in service for children with disabilities, increase misunderstanding and complaints about special education services, and strip 50 years of protections from Minnesota’s special education system.

Important protections for students with disabilities are eliminated in this bill, including:

  • Specific procedures and protections governing suspension and expulsion for students with disabilities, also known as the Minnesota Pupil Fair Dismissal Act (121A.43) and associated rules (Minn. R. 3525.2325)
  • Specialized training for paraprofessionals who will work with students with disabilities (125A.08(c))
  • Parents’ advice on special education programming through Advisory Councils (125A.24)
  • Instruction in Braille for students who are blind and visually impaired that would allow them to communicate at the same level expected of students without disabilities, also known as the Blind Persons’ Literacy Rights and Education Act (125A.06)
  • Specific responsibilities and procedures for cooperation and payment between districts and other agencies, including more than 50 years of legislative history protecting the rights of students with disabilities in schools of parents’ choice (125A.13), foster care (125A.17), and treatment and corrections facilities (125A.52)

Background Information:

  • These eliminations would not reduce any paperwork burden on schools and districts. In fact, removing this guiding language leaves many decisions up to individual school districts. This would be more work, to establish cooperative agreements and processes individually.
  • Minnesota has a historically low level of complaint and due process filings. Removing the language that establishes common understanding of complicated services and cooperative relationships creates uncertainty in the system. This uncertainty will likely lead to an increase in complaints and due process.
  • Much work has been done already to identify Minnesota laws that exceed Federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) guidelines. Two Special Education Legislative Task Forces, in 2007-2008 and 2008-2009, included advocates and educators. The task forces decided that many Minnesota laws were important to keep for children with disabilities and came to consensus decisions to repeal many special education laws.  The 2009 Legislature enacted these consensus decisions and received the Task Forces’ reports.
